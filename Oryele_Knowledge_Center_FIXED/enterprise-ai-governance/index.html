Enterprise AI Governance

Enterprise AI governance establishes who may use AI, for what purposes, with which data and tools, under what controls, and with what evidence of performance and compliance.

Governance starts with inventory

Organizations need a current inventory of AI models, applications, digital workers, data sources, integrations, owners, vendors, and business purposes.

Risk-based controls

Controls should reflect impact. Low-risk drafting assistance does not require the same approvals as financial decisions, regulated communications, legal conclusions, or access to sensitive records.

Identity and least privilege

AI workloads should operate through identifiable service identities with only the permissions required for their assigned work. Credentials, tool access, and data boundaries must be explicit.

Human oversight

Human review should be placed where consequence, uncertainty, or policy requires it. Approval rules should be documented rather than assumed.

Auditability and monitoring

Enterprises need logs of inputs, actions, outputs, approvals, model versions, exceptions, and system interactions. Monitoring should detect drift, errors, unauthorized behavior, and deteriorating outcomes.

Lifecycle management

Governance continues after launch through testing, change control, periodic review, incident response, retirement, and preservation of required records.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does governance slow AI adoption?

Well-designed governance speeds adoption by creating reusable approval paths, standard controls, and confidence for business owners.

What should be logged?

Log the initiating event, relevant inputs, model and prompt version, tools invoked, actions taken, approvals, outputs, exceptions, and final disposition.

Who is accountable for an AI digital worker?

A named business owner should be accountable for its purpose and outcomes, supported by technology, security, legal, privacy, and risk functions.
